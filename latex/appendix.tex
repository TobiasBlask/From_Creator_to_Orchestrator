\section{Plugin Architecture: The Open Paper Machine}
\label{app:plugin}

This appendix documents the \textit{Open Paper Machine specifically}---its plugin structure, orchestration dialogue, and a concrete revision episode. For general background on transformer architecture, RLHF, and Constitutional AI, see Section~\ref{sec:theory}.

The Open Paper Machine is a Claude Code plugin comprising 3{,}938 lines of prompt engineering, orchestration logic, and conversion utilities distributed across 18 files. The complete source is archived at \url{https://github.com/ProfDrT/open-paper-machine}.

\subsection{System Overview}

The plugin consists of four architectural layers:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Plugin manifest} (\texttt{plugin.json}, 18 lines). Declares two MCP server dependencies: \texttt{academic-search} (Semantic Scholar, OpenAlex, CrossRef, arXiv APIs) and \texttt{paperbanana} (AI figure generation via Google Gemini). Both servers start automatically when Claude Code loads the plugin.

  \item \textbf{Autonomous agent} (\texttt{agents/paper-machine.md}, 570 lines). The core orchestration prompt that drives the six-phase pipeline. Operates on five principles: (1)~\textit{Do, don't ask}---make decisions and present results; (2)~\textit{Produce text, not plans}---every phase yields deliverable output; (3)~\textit{Checkpoint, don't block}---present work, then continue; (4)~\textit{Be explicit about decisions}---state what was chosen and why; (5)~\textit{Save everything to files}---every phase produces artifacts.

  \item \textbf{Skill engines} (7 modules, 1{,}933 lines total). Specialized execution modules activated on demand by the agent or user commands. Each encapsulates domain knowledge via fill-in-the-blank templates rather than abstract advice. Table~\ref{tab:skills} summarizes all engines.

  \item \textbf{Command interfaces} (8 commands, 685 lines total). User-facing entry points (\texttt{/write-paper}, \texttt{/search-papers}, \texttt{/export-latex}, \texttt{/verify-citations}, \texttt{/draft-section}, \texttt{/generate-figure}, \texttt{/generate-plot}, \texttt{/respond-reviewers}) that activate specific skill engines or the full pipeline.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\caption{Skill engines of the Open Paper Machine plugin.}
\label{tab:skills}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llp{7.5cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Engine} & \textbf{Lines} & \textbf{Capability} \\
\midrule
writing-engine & 339 & Sentence, paragraph, and section templates for IS/WI/BWL research. Provides concrete fill-in-the-blank formulas (e.g., 6-paragraph introduction blueprint, IMRAD section patterns). \\
literature-engine & 213 & API-first literature discovery across four academic databases. Search strategy generation, concept matrix construction, citation snowballing, narrative synthesis. \\
theory-engine & 169 & Theory selection (TOE, UTAUT, Dynamic Capabilities, etc.), research gap formulation, hypothesis derivation, contribution statement templates. \\
method-engine & 353 & Methodology decision trees for SLR, case study, Gioia, Mayring, grounded theory, PLS-SEM, DSR, and mixed methods. Complete method section templates. \\
figure-engine & 202 & AI figure generation via PaperBanana MCP (Gemini backend) with matplotlib/seaborn fallback. Transforms text descriptions into publication-quality diagrams. \\
latex-engine & 281 & Markdown-to-LaTeX conversion, \texttt{\textbackslash citep}/\texttt{\textbackslash citet} resolution, booktabs tables, arxiv-style compilation. Uses \texttt{md\_to\_latex.py} (732 lines). \\
verification-engine & 376 & Citation verification against source abstracts and full-text. Classifies each citation as VERIFIED, PLAUSIBLE, MISMATCH, UNVERIFIABLE, or NOT FOUND. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Artifact Inventory}

The pipeline produces the following intermediate artifacts, all version-controlled in the repository:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{literature\_base.csv} --- Retrieved literature database (Phase 1)
  \item \texttt{references.bib} --- BibTeX entries for all cited papers (Phase 1)
  \item \texttt{framing.md} --- Theory selection, gap formulation, research questions (Phase 2)
  \item \texttt{concept\_matrix.md} --- Webster \& Watson concept matrix (Phase 3)
  \item \texttt{paper\_structure.md} --- Section architecture with word budget (Phase 3)
  \item \texttt{draft.md} --- Complete paper draft as Markdown (Phase 4)
  \item \texttt{figures/} --- PaperBanana-generated diagrams, 300 DPI PNG (Phase 5)
  \item \texttt{latex/paper.tex} --- arxiv-style LaTeX source (Phase 5)
  \item \texttt{latex/paper.pdf} --- Compiled PDF manuscript (Phase 5)
  \item \texttt{status\_report.md} --- Self-assessed completion status (Phase 6)
  \item \texttt{verification\_report.md} --- Citation verification results (optional Phase 7)
\end{itemize}

\section{Prototypical Orchestrator--Agent Dialogue}
\label{app:dialogue}

The following exchange illustrates the orchestrator--agent interaction pattern during Phase~1 (Reconnaissance). The human provides a research topic; the agent autonomously executes literature search, makes framing decisions, and presents results at a checkpoint. Formatting is lightly edited for readability; the substantive content is verbatim.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\rule{\textwidth}{0.4pt}
\vspace{0.3em}

\noindent\textbf{Orchestrator:}\\
\textit{/write-paper From Creator to Orchestrator: How an LLM Agent Wrote This Paper and What That Means for Science}

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent\textbf{Agent} (Phase 1 --- Reconnaissance):\\
Starting systematic literature search. Generating 6 query variations across theoretical, empirical, and methodological dimensions.

\begin{quote}
\small
\texttt{Query 1:} ``LLM agent academic paper writing automation'' $\rightarrow$ 47 results (Semantic Scholar)\\
\texttt{Query 2:} ``AI-assisted research methodology design science'' $\rightarrow$ 31 results (OpenAlex)\\
\texttt{Query 3:} ``large language model scientific knowledge production'' $\rightarrow$ 52 results (CrossRef)\\
\texttt{Query 4:} ``autonomous AI agent tool use MCP'' $\rightarrow$ 18 results (arXiv)\\
\texttt{Query 5:} ``human-AI collaboration knowledge work orchestration'' $\rightarrow$ 29 results (Semantic Scholar)\\
\texttt{Query 6:} ``generative AI authorship accountability'' $\rightarrow$ 36 results (OpenAlex)
\end{quote}

After deduplication: 148 unique papers. Snowballing on top-5 cited papers added 23 more. Final corpus: 171 candidates.

Screening by title/abstract relevance: 62 papers retained for the concept matrix.

\textbf{Checkpoint:} Literature base saved to \texttt{literature\_base.csv} (171 entries) and \texttt{references.bib} (62 entries). The corpus covers four domains: (1)~LLM capabilities and limitations, (2)~AI-assisted research tools, (3)~automation and knowledge work, (4)~authorship ethics and accountability. Shall I proceed with framing?

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent\textbf{Orchestrator:}\\
\textit{Looks good. Proceed.}

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent\textbf{Agent} (Phase 2 --- Framing):\\
Selected theoretical lenses: (1)~Sociotechnical envelopment \citep{asatiani2021envelopment} for the human--AI accountability structure; (2)~Task redistribution framework \citep{autor2015why,acemoglu2018automation} for analyzing which research tasks shift from human to machine. Design Science Research \citep{hevner2004design} as methodological frame: the paper itself is the artifact; the pipeline is the design.

Research gap: Existing work treats LLMs as either assistive tools or autonomous threats. No study has documented a \textit{complete}, \textit{observable}, \textit{replicable} AI research pipeline while simultaneously theorizing the implications for scientific practice.

\textbf{Checkpoint:} Framing saved to \texttt{framing.md}. Three research questions derived. Proceeding to architecture.

\vspace{0.3em}
\noindent\rule{\textwidth}{0.4pt}
\vspace{0.5em}

This pattern---autonomous execution with checkpoint-based approval---repeats across all six phases. The orchestrator's interventions are evaluative (``Looks good'', ``Redirect the framing toward\ldots'', ``Add a subsection on\ldots'') rather than directive. The agent makes and documents decisions; the orchestrator retains judgment and accountability.

\section{Workflow Example: Correcting an AI-Generated Figure}
\label{app:workflow}

This section documents an actual revision episode from the production of this paper. During co-author review, a conceptual error was identified in Figure~\ref{fig:authorship_framework} (the orchestrator model). The episode illustrates both the iterative nature of human--AI collaboration and the necessity of human oversight for maintaining argumentative coherence.

\subsection{The Error}

The initial version of Figure~6, generated by PaperBanana (Google Gemini backend), contained an arrow labeled ``Accountability'' running from the \textit{AI System} box to the \textit{Published Output} box (Figure~\ref{fig:fig6_flawed}). This directly contradicts the paper's central argument: accountability resides \textit{exclusively} with the human orchestrator. The AI system is a generator---it produces content but cannot bear responsibility for claims. The co-author identified this as a conceptual inconsistency during the review.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/fig10_orchestrator_model_v1_flawed.png}
    \caption{Flawed version of the orchestrator model (v1). Note the ``Accountability'' arrow from the AI System to Published Output---this contradicts the paper's argument that accountability resides solely with the human author.}
    \label{fig:fig6_flawed}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The Correction Dialogue}

The following exchange between orchestrator and agent led to the identification and resolution of the error:

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\rule{\textwidth}{0.4pt}
\vspace{0.3em}

\noindent\textbf{Orchestrator:}\\
\textit{Doesn't the accountability arrow from AI to Output contradict the principle? Explain to me if I'm wrong.}

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent\textbf{Agent:}\\
You are absolutely right---the arrow is misleading. In the figure, an ``Accountability'' arrow goes from the AI System to the Published Output. This contradicts the core argument of the paper: accountability lies \textit{exclusively} with the human orchestrator, never with the AI system. The AI system is only the generator---it cannot bear responsibility.

The correct flow should be:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Human Author $\rightarrow$ (Accountability) $\rightarrow$ Published Output \checkmark
    \item AI System $\rightarrow$ (Artifacts Archived) $\rightarrow$ Contribution Disclosure \checkmark
    \item But AI System $\rightarrow$ (Accountability) $\rightarrow$ Published Output $\times$
\end{itemize}

The arrow should instead be labeled ``Generated Content''---describing the AI's \textit{output contribution}, not accountability.

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent\textbf{Orchestrator:}\\
\textit{Yes! [Regenerate it.]}

\vspace{0.3em}
\noindent\rule{\textwidth}{0.4pt}

\subsection{The Corrected Figure}

The agent regenerated the figure via PaperBanana with explicit instructions that no accountability arrow may originate from the AI System. The corrected version (Figure~\ref{fig:fig6_corrected}) replaces the erroneous ``Accountability'' arrow with ``Generated Content'' and ensures that the only accountability arrow flows from the Human Author to the Published Output.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/fig10_orchestrator_model.png}
    \caption{Corrected version of the orchestrator model (v2). The ``Accountability'' arrow now flows exclusively from the Human Author to the Published Output. The AI System contributes ``Generated Content'' but bears no accountability. Compare with the flawed version in Figure~\ref{fig:fig6_flawed}.}
    \label{fig:fig6_corrected}
\end{figure}

This episode demonstrates three properties of the orchestrator model:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{AI systems can produce conceptually inconsistent artifacts.} The figure generation model (Gemini) optimized for visual clarity but lacked the semantic understanding to recognize that an ``accountability'' arrow from a non-accountable entity violated the paper's argument.

    \item \textbf{Human oversight catches errors that automated systems cannot.} The co-author identified the inconsistency through domain expertise and argumentative reasoning---capabilities that current AI figure generators do not possess.

    \item \textbf{The correction loop is itself an instance of orchestration.} The human identified the problem, the agent confirmed the analysis and proposed the fix, and the human approved the regeneration. This mirrors the Direction $\rightarrow$ Output for Evaluation $\rightarrow$ Direction cycle depicted in the corrected figure itself.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Patterns in the Interaction}

Three patterns emerge from the timeline:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The orchestrator's most consequential interventions are redirections, not corrections.} Interaction \#4 (rejecting the systems framing in favor of the creator-to-orchestrator thesis) shaped the entire paper more than any error correction. This is the ``step up'' strategy \citep{davenport2016just}: the orchestrator operates at the level of research direction, not prose production.

    \item \textbf{Corrections cluster at the semantic level, not the syntactic level.} The agent produces grammatically correct, convention-following prose. Errors occur at the level of \textit{meaning}: hallucinated claims (\#8), conceptual contradictions in figures (\#10), insufficient intellectual risk (\#9). These are precisely the errors that require domain expertise to detect.

    \item \textbf{The interaction character shifts across phases.} Early phases are dominated by \textit{direction} and \textit{expansion} (what to research, how to frame it). Middle phases shift to \textit{correction} and \textit{evaluation} (are the claims right? is the prose sharp enough?). Late phases return to \textit{direction} as co-author feedback introduces new requirements. This pattern---\textit{direct} $\rightarrow$ \textit{evaluate} $\rightarrow$ \textit{redirect}---mirrors the orchestrator cycle depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:authorship_framework}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What the Timeline Reveals}

The timeline operationalizes the ``orchestration is substantive intellectual work'' claim from Section~3.2. Across 14 substantive interactions, the orchestrator:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Made 3 directional decisions that shaped the paper's argument (\#1, \#4, \#13)
    \item Expanded scope based on domain knowledge 2 times (\#2, \#14)
    \item Corrected semantic errors the agent could not self-detect 2 times (\#8, \#10)
    \item Exercised evaluative judgment on quality, balance, and sharpness 4 times (\#5, \#6, \#9, \#12)
\end{itemize}

\noindent None of these interactions involved text production. All involved judgment. This is the creator-to-orchestrator transition made observable.
